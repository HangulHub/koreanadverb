Korean Adverb Conjugation

Welcome to this interactive presentation on Korean adverb conjugation. We'll explore how to form and use adverbs in Korean.

부사 (busa)

Adverb

What are Adverbs?

Adverbs in Korean, like in English, modify verbs, adjectives, or other adverbs. They describe how an action is performed.

In Korean, many adverbs are derived from adjectives, but some are standalone words.

빨리 달리다 (ppalli dallida)

To run quickly

Basic Rules

The basic rule for converting an adjective to an adverb is:

Remove '다' (da) from the adjective and add '게' (ge)

Example: 빠르다 (ppareuda - fast) → 빠르게 (ppareuge - quickly)

그는 빠르게 달립니다.

He runs quickly.

Exceptions

There are some exceptions to this rule:

  1. Adjectives ending in '하다' (hada) → Replace '하다' with '히' (hi)
  2. Some common adverbs have irregular forms
  3. Some adverbs are standalone words and don't derive from adjectives

Usage

Adverbs in Korean typically come before the verb they modify:

빨리 걷다

(ppalli geotda - to walk quickly)

They can also modify adjectives or other adverbs:

매우 크다

(maeu keuda - very big)

Types of Adverbs

Korean adverbs can be categorized into several types:

  • Manner adverbs (how): 천천히cheoncheonhi - slowly
  • Time adverbs: 지금jigeum - now
  • Frequency adverbs: 자주jaju - often
  • Degree adverbs: 매우maeu - very

Adverb Placement

The general rule for adverb placement in Korean is:

Subject + Time Expression + Adverb + Object + Verb

나는 어제 빨리 숙제를 했어요.

(I quickly did my homework yesterday.)

Exception Examples

1. Adjectives ending in '하다' (hada):

  • 조용하다 (joyonghada - quiet) → 조용히 (joyonghi - quietly)
  • 자연스럽다 (jayeonseureopda - natural) → 자연스럽게 (jayeonseureopge - naturally)

2. Irregular forms:

  • 좋다 (jota - good) → 잘 (jal - well)
  • 많다 (manta - many) → 많이 (mani - much)

3. Standalone adverbs:

  • 가끔 (gakkeum - sometimes)
  • 벌써 (beolsseo - already)
